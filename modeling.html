Introduction

Since the structure of TCP2 in Santalum album was unavailable on any database, we ran sequence alignment of TCP2 in Arabidopsis thaliana with the entire sandalwood genome. We got a 73% identity match [1].

Fig: Sequence alignment of TCP2 in A.thaliana with the entire sandalwood genome

To show that the TCP2 gene is not conserved throughout plant organisms, we ran a blast sequence for TCP2 in Arabidopsis thaliana. We checked for the identity percentage with other TCP2 sequences from other plant organisms in NCBI. Results confirmed that the TCP2 gene has high sequence variability throughout plant organism genomes and is not conserved. This confirmed that the gene sequence aligned with TCP2 in Arabidopsis thaliana, hitting a 73% identity, is the TCP2 sequence in sandalwood.

Another blast was done for the potential TCP2 nucleotide sequence and results showed multiple TCP2 genes of various plant organisms with high identity %, confirming the sequence.

The structure for TCP2 in Arabidopsis thaliana (Q93V43) was obtained from UniProt. The structure for SAP11 in Candidatus Phytoplasma asteris was predicted from AlphaFold, using the sequence we obtained from NCBI (WP_342386607.1).

The structure for TCP2 in Santalum album was also predicted using AlphaFold, using the gene sequence we obtained from the sequence alignment. The TCP2 sequence from the sandalwood genome was codon optimised and then the nucleotide sequence was converted to FASTA amino acid sequence.

This FASTA sequence was then put in Swiss Model and AlphaFold predicted, giving us the structure for TCP2 in Santalum album.

Docking

TCP2 in Arabidopsis thaliana and TCP2 in Santalum album were both individually docked with SAP11 in Candidatus Phytoplasma asteris (Asters Yellows Witches broom) to compare the binding affinities. The docking score directly correlates with binding affinity. More negative Gibbs' free energy implies stronger binding. The docking scores are shown below.
