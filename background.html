Background

Our team intends to enhance the overall health of Santalum album by combating its most significant disease, the “Sandalwood Spike Disease” (SSD), caused by the bacterial pathogen Candidatus Phytoplasma asteris, an Aster Yellows phytoplasma (Ayp) which affects the phloem tube cells and causes a multitude of symptoms like leaf dysplasia, floral dysplasia, dwarfism, hormonal imbalance, and overall reduced immunity [1].

Our chassis of choice for this project is Bacillus subtilis, as it is a naturally occurring non-pathogenic bacteria in the environment. This natural association makes B. subtilis a more ecologically balanced option for controlling SSD, caused by Candidatus Phytoplasma asteris, and reduces the risk of disturbing the natural balance of the ecosystem.

B. subtilis is a Gram-positive, spore-forming bacterium known for its robustness in various environmental conditions, making it an ideal candidate for use in agricultural settings.

Our project aims to genetically modify B. subtilis in order to produce our decoy protein, TCP-2 (Teosinte branched 1, cycloidea, and PCF transcription factor 2), a protein in the Arabidopsis thaliana genome. Our designed AtTCP-2 has demonstrated high binding efficiency to SAP11AYWB, an effector protein secreted by Ca. Phytoplasma asteris, thus inhibiting SAP11 activity by preventing its interaction with the Sandalwood plant’s endogenous TCP protein and potentially controlling the spread of SSD [3],[4].

The use of B. subtilis as our chassis aligns with the project's goal of providing an eco-friendly, sustainable alternative to other biocontrol agents while utilizing a microorganism that is both effective and naturally integrated into the Sandalwood plant's biology.
